Read Slower Not Faster For Real Retention

Speed feels productive but memory hates it. Slowing down lets ideas settle in your mind, so you connect new points to what you already know, question the author, and finish with something you can actually use every day.

Write In The Margins Without Apology

A clean book is a forgotten book. Marking pages helps you argue with the text, capture reactions, flag key passages, and turn passive reading into a conversation that stays with you long after you close the cover.

03

Summarise Each Chapter In One Sentence

After every chapter, close the book and say the core idea aloud. Forcing yourself to compress it reveals gaps, strengthens recall, builds understanding, and proves whether you truly grasped the point or just moved your eyes across words.

04

Teach The Idea To Someone Else

Explaining a concept out loud is the fastest test of memory. Teaching forces clarity, exposes confusion, deepens your own learning, and locks the material into place far better than rereading the same highlighted pages twice.

Revisit Your Notes Every Few Weeks

Reading once builds a fragile memory. Coming back to your notes reactivates the ideas, strengthens connections, spots what you forgot, and turns a single read into knowledge you can carry and apply with real confidence.
